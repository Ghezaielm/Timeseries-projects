This study is about predicting future trends (forecasting) using neural networks (LSTMs).

The LSTM model¶

LSTM models inputs has not the same shape than those used in non recurrent neural networks. They take N dimensional vectors as inputs to predict next values. Data are like the scenes of a film. The more we go through the film the best we can predict the end. Good scenarii are less predictable than others. Approching the end of the film, most of us are absorbed by the current scene, forgetting the begining of the film. Cinephiles will take account of the way the author orchestrate each scene, until then end. In that way, good cinephiles are able to predict the end of the film before its end.

Good models are good cinephiles. In the same way, data that are hard to handle are very good films. After having averaged our "scenes", we will try to find a cinephile that is able predict the end of the film with very low error.

Data formatting¶

Here, we define a delay at the end of which the cinephile will say to itself "Okay this scene is getting obvious, this actor will surely die after this". The smaller the delay, the more the cinephile will catch information (risking to pay attention to unsignificant things). But choosing a too long delay will also increase the risk to not pay attention to important features such as recurring actions that will presuppose the end of the film.

Model initialization¶

LSTM (long short term memory) as they are named, can be seen as memory of memories. Why this comparaison ? Because as we know, human memory to do retain the whole details of a concept, we look only for important ones and more if possible. In LSTMs, small neural nets (units) are stacked togather and consists in an input gate (pull the information), a forgot gate (that decide allows to encode only relevant information) and an output gate.

In the following architecture, we choose a 30 unit long LSTM stack to encode the information of the scenes. It could be the number of actors, whether they are talking or not, the place the film category or the main actor state of mind.

As not all the information are relevant to predict the end of the scene, we place a dropout layer which allows us to drop some random information (here 10 % of the stack output). This enforces the model to learn relevant information and converging faster toward a good solution. Then, we add a small unit at the end to ensure a relevant output of the dropout layer. Finally, we add a dense layer with a linear activation as we work with continuous data.

To compile the model, we choose the mean absolute error as our cost function. The metrics used to validate the data is the mean absolute percentage of errors. The optimizer used to improve the convergence of the algorithm during backpropagation is Adam. Its a really cool optimizer when it comes to sparses weights (useful here as we choose a high number of LSTMs cells)

In [325]:
model = Sequential()

model.add(LSTM(units=30,return_sequences=True, input_shape=(features_set.shape[1], 1)))
model.add(Dropout(0.1))
model.add(LSTM(units=1))
model.add(Dense(units = 1))

model.compile(optimizer = 'adam', loss = 'mean_absolute_error',metrics=["mean_absolute_percentage_error"])

Now we can ask a new question :

Does the model/cinephile is able to predict the whole test set. I mean, does the model can make prediction on prediction iteratively and catch relevant information of the end of the film knowning ONLY the begining ?
